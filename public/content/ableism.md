# Ableism

Good to know: Models of Disability/Impairment vs. Disability

Check out next: Temporarily Able Bodied (TAB)/Independent Living (IL)

## Explanation

- Ableism is discrimination/prejudice against people with impairments built upon the assumption that impaired bodies are inferior to their non-impaired counterparts

---

Ableism can be classified through two different angles of categorizations: 

1. **Levels**

| Institutional  | Interpersonal | Internal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ableism that affects organization policy/laws  | Ableism that affects social interactions/relationships  | Ableism that is affects individual perceptions/internalizations |
1. **Forms**

| Hostile | Benevolent | Ambivalent |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Open aggression, oppression and malice | Superficial positive attitudes with underlying biases  | Shifting between the prior two dynamics depending on context |

## Clarification

Ableism is logically false under a social model informed perspective due to:

<aside>
🎭

The impairment/disability distinction:

- **Defining impairments**
    - All bodies are different in traits yet equal in nature
- **Defining disability**
    - Systemic injustice benefit certain/oppress other bodies
    - The illusion of superiority/inferiority is manufactured
</aside>

Runner 1 is boosted by rockets, runner 2 chained by weights, 1 isn’t better than 2 if he runs faster

## Application

1. **Levels**

<aside>
🏘️

Institutional ableism

- Section 14(c) of the FLSA act legally permit organizations to pay workers with disabilities subminimum wages based on the assumption of lower productivity.

Interpersonal ableism

- A coworker speaks to a colleague with autism using a exaggerated “baby voice” despite them having equivalent professional levels as expert engineers

Internal ableism

- People pressuring themselves to suppress the traits of their impairments in order to pass as “normal”
</aside>

1. **Forms**

<aside>
🤬

Hostile ableism

- Able bodied students using “down”, “autistic” or “blind” as derogatory terms for insults in their daily conversations.

Benevolent ableism

- A physically impaired wheelchair user is praised as “brave, heroic and inspiring” for engaging in daily tasks such as shopping groceries

Ambivalent ableism

- A high school teacher praises a student with ADHD for how he “overcomes the odds” yet dismisses his requests for test accommodations as “lazy excuses”.
</aside>
